\chapter{Pricing Methodologies}
\label{chap:analysis}

Having clarified the stochastic differential equations and integrated variance characteristics of the 3/2 volatility model, this chapter will detail the specific numerical algorithms and derivation steps implemented for option pricing. It primarily covers the Monte Carlo simulation frameworks (including conditional and exact simulation) as well as frequency-domain pricing methods based on Fourier transformations.

\section{Monte Carlo Simulation Procedures}
\label{sec:agent_decision_analysis}

Under the 3/2 model, directly discretizing the original SDEs (e.g., using the Euler scheme) often introduces significant truncation errors. Therefore, this study primarily investigates two advanced schemes: conditional Monte Carlo simulation and exact Monte Carlo simulation.

\subsection{Conditional MC}
Conditional Monte Carlo simulation aims to approximate the integrated variance by generating volatility paths. Its primary execution steps are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Path Simulation: Simulate the discrete paths of the volatility process $\sigma_t$ over the time grid $0 \le t_1 \le t_2 \le \dots \le T$, and obtain the terminal volatility $\sigma_T$.
  \item Integral Calculation: Utilize numerical integration rules (such as the Trapezoidal or Simpson’s rule) to perform time integration on the simulated variance paths, thereby obtaining the integrated variance $V_T$ over the entire time period.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Exact MC}
To completely eliminate time discretization errors, the exact simulation method directly samples the terminal states without relying on the step-by-step generation of paths. Its core steps are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Terminal Variance Simulation: Directly simulate the terminal variance $V_T$ using the known transition density of the variance process under the 3/2 model.
  \item Conditional Integrated Variance Sampling: Conditional on the known $v_T$ (or equivalently, the terminal volatility $\sigma_T$), sample the integrated variance $V_T$ from the numerical cumulative distribution function (CDF) of $V_T|v_T$. This step typically involves complex inner-layer Inverse Laplace transform operations.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Common Procedures for Pricing and Aggregation}
Regardless of whether the integrated variance is obtained via conditional MC or exact MC, the subsequent steps for calculating the option price are universal:
3. Parameter Transformation: Calculate the expected value of the underlying asset conditional on given $v_T$ and $V_T$, denoted as $E(S_T|v_T, V_T)$, and determine the effective volatility, usually expressed as $\rho^*\sqrt{V_T/T}$.
1. Option Valuation:
  * Approach 1 (Price Sampling): Draw samples from the lognormal or normal distribution to calculate the terminal option payoff.
  * Approach 2 (Analytical Formula): Directly substitute the modified parameters into the Bachelier or Black-Scholes-Merton (BSM) option pricing formulas. Specifically, set the modified initial price as $S_0 := E(S_T|v_T)$ and the modified volatility as $\sigma := \rho^*\sqrt{V_T/T}$.
1. Averaging: Average the resulting option prices across all independent simulation paths to obtain the final expected present value of the option.

\section{Fourier Transformation Procedures}
Beyond time-domain Monte Carlo simulations, frequency-domain methods (Fourier transformations) provide another highly efficient pricing avenue, provided that the characteristic function is known.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Fast Fourier Transform (FFT): By converting the option pricing formula into an integral in the Fourier space, FFT can simultaneously compute option prices across multiple strike prices, significantly enhancing computational speed. However, when dealing with options that are near expiration, the oscillatory nature of the integrand leads to pricing failures or severe degradation in accuracy.
    \item Fourier-Cosine Transformation (COS Method): To overcome the aforementioned drawbacks, this study specifically introduces and examines the pricing method based on the conditional Fourier-Cosine transform. This method approximates the target probability density function through a cosine series expansion. Both theoretical analysis and subsequent numerical results demonstrate that the Fourier-Cosine transform not only maintains a relatively fast computational speed but also achieves precise option pricing under far more extreme parameter conditions.
\end{enumerate}
