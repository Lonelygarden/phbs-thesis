\chapter{Contributions and Conclusion}
\label{chap:conclusion}

\section{Core Innovations and Mathematical Contributions}

Addressing the complexity of option pricing under the 3/2 volatility model, this study not only conducted systematic comparisons at the algorithm application level but also achieved substantial progress in underlying mathematical derivations. The core innovative contributions of this paper are primarily reflected in the following three aspects:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item In-depth Analysis of the Modified Bessel Function: The core difficulty in exact simulation methods often lies in handling complex special functions. This study systematically analyzed the first-order partial and second-order derivative of the Modified Bessel Function of the first kind.
    \item Rigorous Derivation of Convergence Conditions: Building upon the systematic study of the derivatives and properties of the aforementioned Bessel function, this study successfully derived and identified the explicit convergence conditions. The perfection of this underlying theory provides a solid mathematical guarantee for the stability of subsequent algorithms.
    \item Proposal of an All-Scenario Fast Pricing Method: Based on these theoretical breakthroughs, this paper presents a novel, fast option pricing method. This method successfully overcomes the defects of traditional exact simulation and Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) when facing extreme scenarios, effectively covering various extreme scenarios under the 3/2 volatility model.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Empirical Findings and Research Conclusions}

By constructing test cases encompassing seven combinations of normal and extreme parameters (Case I to Case VII), this study conducted exhaustive numerical comparative analyses on five existing mainstream pricing methods alongside the improved framework proposed herein.

The research findings profoundly reveal the trade-offs between computational accuracy and efficiency across different pricing algorithms. Specifically:

* The Euler/Milstein scheme, while universally applicable, is extremely slow.
* Traditional exact simulation and almost exact simulation are highly prone to severe deviations or outright failures when processing near-expiration or ATM options.
* The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), despite being exceptionally fast, suffers from massive numerical collapse in near-expiration scenarios.

In conclusion, this study confirms that the proposed approach optimally balances the contradiction between precision and speed, thereby identifying the optimal choice for different application scenarios. The various findings of this research not only enrich the pricing theory of stochastic volatility models but also provide explicit guidance and optimal solutions for algorithm selection in diverse practical applications (such as high-frequency trading and risk management) within financial institutions.
