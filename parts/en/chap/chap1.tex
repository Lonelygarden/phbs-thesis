\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background and Significance}

Option pricing has always been a core subject in the field of financial engineering. In the traditional framework of financial asset pricing, the Black-Scholes (BS) model has occupied a dominant position due to its elegant analytical form. However, a fundamental assumption of the BS model is that the volatility of the underlying asset remains constant. With the continuous development of financial markets and the deepening of empirical research, researchers and practitioners have observed that the asset volatility in real-world markets often exhibits significant stochastic characteristics. This stochastic nature of asset volatility introduces profound complexity and formidable challenges to the modeling of financial markets.

To overcome the limitations of the constant volatility assumption, academia has proposed a series of stochastic volatility models. In recent years, the 3/2 volatility model has gradually entered the vision of scholars and attracted widespread attention. Unlike the traditional 1/2 models (such as the Heston model), the 3/2 model assumes that volatility evolves dynamically over time and follows an inverse 3/2 power law. This non-linear dynamic specification enables the 3/2 model to better capture the drastic fluctuations in volatility under extreme market stress and more accurately fit the \enquote{volatility smile} phenomenon observed in empirical market data.

\section{Pricing Challenges under the 3/2 Volatility Model}

Although the 3/2 model demonstrates significant advantages in characterizing real market dynamics, its intricate mathematical structure also brings tremendous computational hurdles to option pricing. Currently, there are five mainstream option pricing methods under the 3/2 model framework, yet they all exhibit respective limitations in practical applications:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Euler/Milstein scheme: This is a highly generalized numerical method, but its convergence rate is notoriously slow, resulting in poor computational efficiency.
    \item Exact simulation: Theoretically, this method can completely eliminate time discretization errors, but it suffers from sluggish computation speeds. More critically, under certain specific parameter combinations, it fails to price at-the-money (ATM) options or options near expiration.
    \item Almost exact simulation: Although it offers improvements in computational speed, this method similarly fails to effectively price ATM options and options far from expiration under some specific parameter settings.
    \item Exact simulation based on conditional Fourier-cosine Method: This method breaks down under several specific parameter combinations, failing to fulfill the option pricing task.
    \item Fast Fourier Transform (FFT): Despite its outstanding performance in calculation speed, the FFT method possesses inherent flaws when dealing with the pricing of options near expiration.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Objective and Main Content}

Given the ineffectiveness or inefficiency of the aforementioned existing pricing methods under specific parameter combinations (especially extreme market scenarios), this study is dedicated to systematically evaluating and refining option pricing techniques under the 3/2 model.

Initially, this research replicates and implements the five mainstream pricing methods discussed above, conducting pricing tests on vanilla options under parameter combinations representing various market scenarios. Through comparative analysis of extensive numerical results, this paper meticulously reveals the trade-offs between computational accuracy and efficiency across different methods, clarifying their respective advantages and disadvantages.

More importantly, addressing the deficiencies of existing methods in pricing ATM and near-expiration options, this study proposes a novel, high-precision option pricing methodology. This proposed method can not only be widely applied to various normal and extreme market scenarios but also achieve relatively fast computational speeds while maintaining exceptional accuracy.
